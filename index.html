Malaysia’s Meat Dependency

Poultry dominates Malaysia’s meat basket, while beef and mutton show a much stronger dependence on imports.

Main question

Is Malaysia eating more meat than it can reliably produce?

The data shows a clear split: poultry is Malaysia’s main meat and remains relatively close to domestic supply strength, while beef and mutton depend heavily on imports. This creates a supply risk if global prices, trade routes or exporting countries are disrupted.

Malaysia sits within Southeast Asia

This map places Malaysia in its regional context before focusing on Malaysia’s internal meat pattern. It helps the reader understand that Malaysia is part of a wider food trade region, but the main story is about Malaysia’s own meat balance.

Reading the story

The page is organised from demand to supply risk. It first shows what Malaysians consume, then compares local production with domestic use, and finally highlights which meats are most exposed to imports.

Key terms

Self-sufficiency ratio: how much domestic production can cover local demand.

Import dependency ratio: how much supply relies on imports.

Projection: a simple estimated extension, not official data.

Beef and mutton carry the highest supply risk

This heatmap compares each meat type across four simple risk indicators. The scores are derived from official 2024 consumption, self-sufficiency and import dependency data. Darker cells show a stronger risk signal.

Demand is concentrated in poultry

Malaysia’s selected meat basket is not evenly spread across meat types. In 2024, poultry consumption was far ahead of pork, beef and mutton. This makes poultry central to everyday food demand, while beef and mutton are smaller in quantity but more vulnerable because local supply covers much less of domestic use.

This is why the project does not only ask “what meat is eaten most?”. It also asks “which meat creates the bigger supply risk?”

Poultry takes the largest share of Malaysia’s meat basket

This stacked area chart shows the changing share of each meat type in total selected meat consumption. The basket remains strongly poultry-heavy, meaning changes in poultry supply can affect a large part of the overall meat system.

Farming the demand

Poultry has the strongest domestic position among the selected meats, but it is still not completely risk-free. The 2024 data shows poultry is much closer to the 100% self-sufficiency line than beef and mutton, yet its large consumption volume means even a small supply gap matters.

The ruminant problem

Beef and mutton show a different pattern. They are consumed in smaller amounts, but their self-sufficiency ratios are very low. This means Malaysia depends much more on external supply for these meats, making them more exposed to import price and availability changes.

Poultry is much closer to self-sufficient than beef and mutton

Self-sufficiency remains uneven across meat types

Beef and mutton show the clearest production-utilization gap

This chart compares domestic production with domestic use. A larger gap suggests stronger reliance on external supply or imports. Beef and mutton show this risk more clearly than poultry.

Imports bridge the gap

Trade data explains how Malaysia fills the domestic supply gap. Imports are especially important for beef and mutton because local production covers only a small part of domestic use. In contrast, poultry has a stronger domestic base, although it still has import and export movement.

Why this matters

A meat type with low local supply and high import dependency is more vulnerable to global disruption. If import costs rise or supply routes are affected, beef and mutton are more exposed than poultry.

The food security risk is concentrated in beef and mutton

This quadrant chart combines consumption, self-sufficiency and import dependency using 2024 official data. Beef and mutton sit lower on self-sufficiency and higher on import dependency, placing them in the higher-risk area.

Malaysia’s selected meat basket is overwhelmingly poultry-heavy

This custom waffle chart translates the 2024 official consumption pattern into 100 simple units. It makes the dominance of poultry easier to understand at a glance and acts as a simple visual summary of the whole story.

Final takeaway

Malaysia’s meat system has two different risks

The first risk is scale: poultry dominates consumption, so it matters most to everyday demand. The second risk is dependency: beef and mutton are much more import-dependent, even though they are consumed less. A strong food security response must therefore protect both high-volume poultry supply and vulnerable ruminant supply chains.
